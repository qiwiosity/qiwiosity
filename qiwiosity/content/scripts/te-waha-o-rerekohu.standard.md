# Te Waha-o-Rerekohu — standard (95–115s)

In the grounds of Te Araroa Area School, on the far eastern edge of the North Island, there's a tree that's hard to take in all at once. You have to walk around it, twice, before the size resolves. Pōhutukawa branches don't grow upward — they grow outward and down, making their own shade, making their own forest. This one has a branch span of over thirty-seven metres, the largest ever recorded for the species.

Its name is Te Waha-o-Rerekohu. "The mouth of Rerekohu." Rerekohu was a rangatira of this rohe, and in tradition, kūmara was stored in a pit beneath the tree — the tree was his pātaka, his food store. Its roots are the floor of that store still.

The tree is at least six hundred years old. One study places its germination around the year 1421, which would make it around 600, give or take two centuries. That means it was growing here before Abel Tasman, before Cook, before the British Empire existed, before Te Araroa had its current name. It's older than Christchurch. It might be older than the printing press. It's certainly older than the Dutch Golden Age.

Pōhutukawa is called the New Zealand Christmas tree — for its blood-red flowers in December and January. To see this one in bloom is to see something almost too much for a single photograph: a cliff of red above the school fence.

The tree is on school grounds but is publicly accessible, and visitors are asked to treat it with the care and quiet it deserves. Don't climb. Don't break branches for souvenir. Stand back a few metres and look up.

Just to the north, around the corner, the East Cape Lighthouse watches the first sunrise in the world. Plan to be there at dawn and here by breakfast.
