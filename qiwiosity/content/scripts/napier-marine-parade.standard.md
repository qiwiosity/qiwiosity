# Marine Parade / Art Deco Napier — standard (90–120s)

On the third of February, 1931, at 10:47 in the morning, a magnitude 7.8 earthquake hit Hawke's Bay. It lasted two and a half minutes. By the time it stopped, Napier had flattened. Two hundred and fifty-six people were dead — most from collapsing chimneys, masonry, and the fires that followed.

The town started rebuilding within weeks. And here's the unlikely part: they chose Art Deco. It was the absolute newest style in the world in 1931 — cinemas in New York had it, ocean liners had it, and now a small New Zealand port town in the middle of the Depression decided it would have it too. The architects worked fast and cheap: reinforced concrete, flat roofs, geometric sunbursts, and a deliberate decision to keep everything low and horizontal — partly as earthquake engineering, partly because the stripped-back, forward-looking lines suited a town that wanted nothing more to do with the Victorian facades that had just killed its citizens.

By 1933 most of the CBD was back. The reason it's still here — and why you can walk along Emerson Street and Tennyson Street and Marine Parade today and see something almost unchanged in ninety years — is that Napier never got rich enough to tear it all down and start again, the way Auckland and Wellington did. What saved it was neglect. Then, in the 1980s, a small group of locals realised what they had and started organising to protect it. Today the Napier Art Deco Trust runs daily walking tours, and the town's annual Art Deco Festival in February is one of the biggest heritage events in the country.

If you only do one thing: walk the block between Emerson and Tennyson after dark, when the neon signs are lit. That's the Napier everyone comes here to see.
