# Māhia Peninsula — standard (120–140s)

Māhia is shaped like a fist punched out into the Pacific. Twenty-one kilometres long, eleven wide, high ground at the southern tip, sheltered bays on the west. For anyone arriving by waka, it was the best landfall on this coast — calm water on one side, deep ocean on the other, a high point to see storms coming.

The full name is Te Māhia-mai-tawhiti, which translates roughly as "the sound heard from a distance". This is the homeland of Ngāti Rongomaiwahine — an iwi who trace their whakapapa to Rongomaiwahine, a high-ranking ancestor descended from both the Tākitimu and Kurahaupō canoes. Older still, tradition holds that Whatonga, grandson of the navigator Toi, settled here after arriving from Hawaiki. Toi followed, looking for him. Whatonga stayed.

For a thousand years Māhia was fishing, gardens, and deep cultural authority. In the 1830s it became — briefly — a shore whaling station. By the late nineteenth century it was back to being a farming peninsula that most of New Zealand forgot about. That's roughly where it sat for a hundred years.

Then, in 2016, something unusual happened. A small New Zealand aerospace company called Rocket Lab, founded by Peter Beck, was looking for a private launch site with a clear southern arc over empty ocean, minimal air traffic, and an agreeable local landowner. Māhia had all three. The company leased land from Tawapata South Incorporation — a Māori-owned land block — and built Launch Complex 1 on the southern tip. On the 21st of January 2018, a rocket called *Still Testing* reached orbit. It was the first orbital launch from New Zealand, and the first successful orbital launch from a private spaceport anywhere in the world.

So: Māhia is whaling station, rocket launch pad, surfing beach, quiet bach community, and living rohe of Rongomaiwahine, all stacked on the same peninsula. Drive the loop road slowly. Stop at Mahanga Beach. If you're very lucky, you'll see a rocket trail in the sky at dusk.
