# Waipunga Falls — standard (95–115s)

There's a lookout coming up on your right in a few hundred metres. Take it. It's one of the easiest stops on this road, and one of the prettiest — Waipunga Falls, a forty-metre, three-tiered drop straight off the edge of the highway.

The river you're looking at is the Waipunga. It rises in the Kaimanawa Range to the west — rough, high country on the edge of the central volcanic plateau — and drains east to join the Mohaka, the big river you crossed further back. The falls themselves sit in a narrow pumice gorge. The top tier is the tallest; below it the water steps through two lower drops before disappearing into bush. In winter after heavy rain the whole thing thunders. In summer it narrows to a clean white ribbon.

What makes this stop unusual is that you don't have to walk anywhere. The lookout is literally built for drivers — a short barrier at the edge of the highway, a view across the valley straight onto the falls, and enough room to stretch your legs for a minute. In a country where most of the big waterfalls demand a tramp, this one gives itself up for free.

The bush you're looking across is the remains of a much bigger native forest that once covered everything from here up to Lake Taupō. The survey parties cutting the Napier–Taupō coach road in the 1870s described forest so dense they could only make out the sky directly above the track. Most of it is gone now, cleared for pasture a century ago. This gorge is part of what's left.

If you're travelling with kids, this is the toilet-stop-and-a-photo combination the drive needs. If you're on your own, stand a minute. Look. It'll still be falling when you go.
