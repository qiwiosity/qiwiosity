# Esk Valley — standard (115–135s)

The Esk Valley is the first thing you cross leaving Napier on SH5. Flat, green, wine country — Hawke's Bay's northernmost sub-region, and for a long time one of its quietest. That changed in six hours on the morning of the 14th of February, 2023.

Cyclone Gabrielle had been tracking down the North Island for two days. By the evening of the 13th the rain was relentless, and by around four the next morning the Esk River — normally a braided stream with a sleepy mouth — was a wall of brown water moving fast. It jumped its banks. It went across the valley floor. Within two hours, houses were under eaves. Vineyards were buried in silt up to two metres deep. Cars were floating.

Eleven people died in Hawke's Bay that day, across the region. The Esk Valley lost homes, livestock, and a significant portion of its 2023 vintage. At Petane Wines — a small family operation on the north side — winemaker Philip Barber dug, by hand, twelve thousand bottles out of ten feet of silt. At Sacred Hill, fifteen hectares of their Dartmoor vineyard simply ceased to exist: the vines are under a permanent new riverbed. Down the valley at Linden Estate, six hectares of mature vines went under silt, and the cellar door was torn apart.

What you're driving through now is the slow work of coming back. Some vineyards have replanted in the same rows. Some have moved uphill. A few have sold up. The winery cellar doors along the SH5 loop — Linden, Crab Farm, and across at Church Road in the other direction — have been among the more visible symbols of recovery, partly because locals have gone out of their way to visit and buy.

If you're looking for somewhere to stop: Linden Estate is a good start, but follow the local signs. Recovery is uneven, and a year-old list of what's open can be wrong.

A last thing to notice on the drive out of the valley: the highway itself had to be rebuilt in several places. The piled-up shoulder and fresh tarmac near Eskdale are not aesthetic. They're the scar.
