# Huka Falls — standard (95–115s)

Just off SH1, a few minutes north of Taupō town, the Waikato River does something remarkable. For most of its length — all four hundred and twenty-five kilometres of it — the Waikato is a wide, slow-moving river, the biggest in New Zealand. But at Huka Falls, it is forced through a narrow canyon only fifteen metres across.

The river upstream is about a hundred metres wide and four metres deep. The canyon is a hundred and thirty metres long, ten to fifteen metres wide, and the water has to drop eleven metres through it before spilling off the lip at the end. Two hundred and twenty cubic metres of water are moving through this gap every single second — an Olympic-size swimming pool every eleven seconds. It is the largest single-channel waterfall in New Zealand by flow volume, and one of the highest-volume river constrictions on the planet.

The rock it has carved is ignimbrite — compressed pumice and ash from the Oruanui eruption twenty-five thousand years ago. That material is hard enough to resist the river but soft enough to show the scouring. The walls of the canyon are grooved and pocked from the work the water has been doing for millennia. The water itself glows an intense pale blue. That colour is the Waikato's normal colour — turquoise from lake Taupō, amplified by foam and sunlight in the canyon.

"Huka" means foam. It's the right word. The viewing bridge sits directly above the final drop and the spray, if the wind is right, will reach you.

Practical note: the best viewpoint is the footbridge, and the walk down to the base from the lower carpark takes five minutes and is worth doing. The Huka Falls walkway also continues up to Aratiatia, three hours on foot along a beautiful stretch of river. If you only have twenty minutes, the bridge will do.
