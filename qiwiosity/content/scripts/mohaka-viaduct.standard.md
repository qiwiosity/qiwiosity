# Mōhaka Viaduct — standard (100–125s)

The Mōhaka River cuts a dramatic cleft between northern Hawke's Bay and Wairoa — steep, deep, and awkward. In the 1920s, if you wanted to push a railway line up to Gisborne, you had to cross it. No detour was cheaper.

So the Public Works Department designed one of the most ambitious pieces of engineering in New Zealand history. The plan, by engineers John Lelliot Cull and William Langston Newnham, was a steel-truss viaduct ninety-five metres tall and nearly two hundred and seventy-seven metres long — the tallest railway viaduct in the southern hemisphere, then and now.

Construction started in 1930. Then the story gets harder. The Great Depression hit, money ran out, and work was abandoned in 1931 — the same year the Hawke's Bay earthquake tore through the region and damaged the half-finished line. For five years the unfinished piers sat in the gorge.

Work resumed in 1936. When it did, the pace was remarkable: the viaduct was completed in about a year and opened in July 1937. Crossing it by train is still one of the great rail experiences in New Zealand — a long, slow, very high glide above the river. It's a Category 1 Heritage New Zealand listing.

You're most likely seeing it from State Highway 2 at Raupunga, where there's a pull-off with a clear view of the whole structure. The scale isn't obvious until you spot a car on the road below it. Watch for a few minutes. A log train might go over. The steel sings.

A quick note before you drive on: the river below you is the Mōhaka — a taonga river to Ngāti Pāhauwera, who have long fought for its protection. The viaduct is engineering history. The river is older, and more alive.
