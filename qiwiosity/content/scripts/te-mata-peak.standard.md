# Te Mata Peak / Te Mata o Rongokako — standard (90–120s)

The ridgeline here has a shape people notice — long, low, unmistakably human. Head at the northern end, chest rising toward the summit, feet tapering south. In Ngāti Kahungunu tradition this is the body of the giant Rongokako, and the peak itself is his face — Te Mata o Rongokako.

The story goes that Rongokako fell in love with the daughter of Te Whatu-i-āpiti, a chief whose people lived on these plains. To prove his love, he was set a series of tasks — the last of which was to eat his way through the hill. He tried. And here, near the summit, he choked. The ridge you see is where his body fell.

The land was gifted to the people of Hastings in 1927 by the Chambers family, and Te Mata Park has been protected ever since. A mountain-bike park runs the lower slopes; the short summit walk from the car park is about twenty minutes on tarmac, genuinely flat until the last rise. The long walk up from Tauroa Road is ninety minutes through redwood and pine, and worth it.

On a clear day the view opens all the way from the Ruahine Range in the west to Hawke's Bay in the east — the bite of the fish hook. Paragliders launch off the eastern face in late afternoon. Go at dawn if you want the silence. Go at sunset if you want the colour.

A note before you walk up — this is a living ancestor, not a theme park. Please stay on the marked tracks.
