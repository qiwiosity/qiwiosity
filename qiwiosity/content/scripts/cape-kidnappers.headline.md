# Cape Kidnappers / Te Kauwae-a-Māui — headline (25s)

This cape has two names, and two stories. To Māori, it's Te Kauwae-a-Māui — the jawbone Māui used to fish up the North Island. To Cook, in October 1769, it was the cape where local Māori tried to rescue a boy from his ship. Today, six and a half thousand pairs of gannets nest on the cliff in front of you.

---

**Voice direction:** unrushed, almost a storyteller's pace. Pause after "two stories." Lift gently on "fish up the North Island." Land the gannet number quietly — the point is that two epic stories coexist with a very alive wildlife spectacle.

**Runtime target:** 22–28 seconds at ~150 wpm.

**Fact-check:**
- Te Kauwae-a-Māui = "the jawbone of Māui" ✅ LINZ / Te Ara.
- Māui fished up Te Ika-a-Māui (North Island) using a hook made from the jawbone of his grandmother Murirangawhenua — the cape's curve is said to be the hook. ✅
- Cook / Endeavour encounter: 15 October 1769. The abduction attempt was on Taiata, the young Tahitian servant of Tupaia. ✅
- Local tradition (Ngāi Te Whatuiāpiti under Te Rangikoianake): Māori believed Taiata was a captive being held and tried to rescue him.
- 6,500+ pairs of Australasian gannets — ✅ current DOC estimate (varies year to year).
