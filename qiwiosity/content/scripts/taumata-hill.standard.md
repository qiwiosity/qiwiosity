# Taumata hill — standard (110–130s)

You're looking at an unremarkable hill. Three hundred and five metres high. Sheep on it. But the sign on the fence at the base is one of the most photographed objects in New Zealand, because it carries this name:

Taumata­whakatangi­hanga­koauau­o­Tamatea­turi­pukaka­piki­maunga­horo­nuku­pokai­whenua­ki­tana­tahu.

Eighty-five letters. The longest place name in any English-speaking country, and by most measures the longest place name in the world. Guinness has officially recognised it since the 1980s.

The name is not random. It's a story, compressed. Tamatea — Tamatea-pōkai-whenua, "Tamatea who encircled the land" — was one of the great early explorers of Aotearoa, whose travels are woven into the whakapapa of iwi up and down the east coast. The name of this hill translates, roughly, as "the summit where Tamatea — the man with the big knees, who climbed mountains, was thrown about by storms, and travelled across the land — played his kōauau to his beloved". In the fuller tradition, Tamatea had been in battle in this area and lost his brother. He stayed, mourning. He played his kōauau — a small bone flute, held between the lips — for his brother, and for the woman he loved. The melody named the hill.

A longer, 105-letter version of the name exists in some recordings, with even more of Tamatea's story packed in. The sign at the road carries the 85-letter version because it's the one that stuck.

The hill itself is on private farmland, so the sign is all most visitors see. That's enough. It's not really a hill. It's a poem, standing on a fence line.

If you're driving the detour down to Pōrangahau from SH2, it's about twenty minutes each way. Pōrangahau itself is a small Ngāti Kere community with a beautiful coastal surf break worth the extra half hour.
