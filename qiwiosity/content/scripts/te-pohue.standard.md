# Te Pōhue — standard (95–115s)

Te Pōhue isn't much — a scattered township around a small lake, about fifty-five kilometres north of Napier, perched on the climb before the Titiokura Saddle. But every square metre of it has a story, because this was where the Cobb & Co coach stopped for lunch.

The first hotel was built here around 1869, just as surveyors were cutting the Napier–Taupō coach road through what was then dense native forest. When the coach service launched on the first of January 1872, Te Pōhue became the mid-morning change-of-horses and feed stop — an essential piece of infrastructure for anyone trying to get from the port at Napier to the interior of the central North Island in a single day.

The hotel had an unlucky run. The first one was replaced, and the second burnt down in 1897 — sparks from a farmer clearing scrub nearby landed on the shingle roof, and the whole thing went up. A third hotel, the Lake Hotel, was built in 1899 by Joseph King on a prime site overlooking the lake. It lasted thirty-two years. Then, at ten forty-seven on the morning of the third of February 1931, the Hawke's Bay earthquake arrived. The Lake Hotel came down.

What you see today is what the township became after that — a rural crossroads with a small school, a fire station, a few homes, and, crucially, a highway servo which has saved a lot of drivers with a dodgy fuel gauge.

The road out of Te Pōhue climbs steeply. In the pre-war truck era, transport companies used to stash spare gearboxes at the summits up ahead because the climbs were famous for wrecking them. When you drop to second gear on the next few corners, you're driving the same hills.
