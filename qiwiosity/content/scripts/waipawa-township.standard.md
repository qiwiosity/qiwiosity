# Waipawa — standard (100–125s)

Long before it had a main street, this was a crossing. The Waipawa River runs out of the Ruahine Range, and anyone moving north–south on the eastern side of the North Island had to ford it somewhere. Ngāti Kahungunu — specifically the hapū of Ngāi Toroiwaho and Ngāti Whatuiāpiti — had lived on these plains for centuries, with kainga, gardens, and eel weirs up and down the river.

The European chapter starts in 1851. Government agent Donald McLean negotiated the purchase of three huge blocks of Hawke's Bay land — more than 250,000 hectares in total — from Ngāti Kahungunu. That transaction opened Central Hawke's Bay to sheep runholders, and in 1860 a runholder named Frederick Abbott laid out a small town next to the ford. He called it Abbottsford. The other settlers preferred the Māori name. Within a decade the town was Waipawa on every map.

What makes it worth a stop now is what didn't happen. Waipawa never boomed. It was a busy provincial service town from the 1870s to about the 1960s, and then it politely stopped changing. Walk the main street and you'll see wooden shopfronts, a 1920s post office, the old Central Hawke's Bay County Council chambers, and one of the last genuinely in-use small-town pubs in the country. The CHB Settlers Museum, a few minutes away, has the local side of the story — photos, farming gear, the whole pioneering scaffold.

There are two marae in the town — Mataweka and Nohomaiterangi — affiliated with the same hapū who've been here all along. You don't visit them casually. But you do drive past them, and it's worth knowing why they're there.

Practical: coffee at the Red Bridge café, lunch at the Waipawa Hotel, and a quick detour south to the Tukituki River bridge if you like unfashionable New Zealand landscapes.
