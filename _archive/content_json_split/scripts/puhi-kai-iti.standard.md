# Puhi Kai Iti / Cook Landing Site — standard (120–140s)

Stand at the mouth of the Tūranganui River and face the sea. This is probably the most historically loaded square kilometre in New Zealand.

The story that gets most of the attention is Cook's. On the seventh of October 1769, a twelve-year-old named Nicholas Young — "Young Nick" — spotted land from the Endeavour's masthead. Two days later, on the ninth, Cook and a small party rowed ashore and became the first Europeans to set foot on New Zealand soil. What followed was not the handshake the Endeavour's journals imagined. Over the first two days, nine Māori were shot and killed. The first was Te Maro, a rangatira of Ngāti Oneone. Cook couldn't get the supplies he'd hoped for, called the bay "Poverty Bay", and sailed south.

But the older story is the one the place has always carried. Centuries earlier — around the 1200s or 1300s — the waka Horouta landed in this same bay, captained by Kīwa. Kīwa settled, and the harbour became Tūranganui-a-Kiwa — "the great standing-place of Kīwa". Another waka captain, Māia, built a pā at Kaiti Beach called Puhi Kai Iti. You're standing on, or very near, its site. The people you can't see — Ngāti Oneone, Rongowhakaata, Te Aitanga-a-Māhaki and others — never left. They've been here twenty generations longer than the story in the plaque.

The way the site is interpreted has changed enormously. For decades the monuments here told the Cook story only. After the 250th anniversary in 2019 — Tuia 250 — that changed. Today at Puhi Kai Iti / Cook Landing Site you'll find, alongside the original 1906 Cook obelisk, a set of pou carved by local iwi artists, a re-named reserve, and signage that tells both stories side by side. Up the hill at Titirangi / Kaiti Hill there's a memorial to Te Maro at the Ruatanuika lookout.

Walk here slowly. It's small. But you can feel how much history is pressed into it.
