# East Cape Lighthouse — standard (105–125s)

To get here you drive the unsealed final stretch of State Highway 35 around the eastern tip of the North Island — a road most New Zealanders have never driven. The cliffs get taller. The farms get more remote. And at the end, you park at the bottom of a steep hill with a wooden staircase running up it.

Roughly seven hundred steps. On a good day it takes fifteen minutes. On a dawn climb in winter, with head torches, it takes twenty, and feels much longer.

At the top, the lighthouse. A white tower, a small fenced yard, and the Pacific in every direction. This is the easternmost lighthouse in the world.

Two things about that. First: there's a gentle Kiwi argument to have about "first sunrise". In 1995, the nation of Kiribati shifted the international dateline east to put its whole territory on one side of it, which made Caroline Island technically the first inhabited place to see each new day. Pedants will tell you East Cape lost the title then. Geographically, because of the curvature of the earth and the tilt of its axis, East Cape still sees the sun touch land before any other point in the world — for most of the year, anyway. You can have the debate. The sunrise doesn't care.

Second thing: the lighthouse didn't start here. The original light was built in 1900 on East Island, a small rocky island a couple of kilometres offshore. The island turned out to be geologically unstable — landslides kept threatening the lightkeepers' cottages. So in 1922 the whole thing was moved, tower and all, to this hilltop on the mainland. Three keepers lived here until automation in 1985. Now it's just the light and the wind.

Practical: bring a torch and more clothing than you think you need. It's always colder up there than it looks. And on the drive back, stop at Te Araroa for the giant pōhutukawa tree. Best breakfast you'll ever earn.
