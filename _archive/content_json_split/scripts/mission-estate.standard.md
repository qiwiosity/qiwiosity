# Mission Estate — standard (110–130s)

A group of French Marist priests arrived in Hawke's Bay in 1851 with a mission, a lot of faith, and — because they needed sacramental wine and knew from southern France how to make it — a few vine cuttings. They settled first at Pakowhai, near the Ngaruroro River. They planted gardens, kept cattle, and put in vines. That's the founding moment of New Zealand's oldest winery, and also, more or less, the founding moment of the Hawke's Bay wine industry.

It almost didn't survive. In the 1850s, inter-iwi conflict made the original site untenable, and the mission moved to Meeanee in 1858. Then in 1897 came a biblical flood that buried the vineyards in silt. By 1909, they'd decided to move again — this time to higher ground at Greenmeadows, where you are now.

The move is the story everyone tells. The mission building itself — a large wooden seminary — was cut into eleven sections, placed on rollers, and dragged five kilometres uphill by traction engines. It took two days. People rode on top of it.

The building survived the move. Twenty-two years later, on the third of February 1931, it survived the earthquake, too — but not everyone inside did. Two priests and seven seminarians were at prayer in the stone chapel when the quake hit. The chapel collapsed. All nine were killed. There is a memorial on the site.

Today, Mission Estate is still a working winery, with a cellar door, restaurant, and one of the region's grand heritage buildings. The Mission Concert — an annual summer event — is one of the biggest outdoor concerts in New Zealand. But the deepest thing here is the continuity. A hundred and seventy-five years of people, in the same valley, making wine in a tradition that predates almost everything else you can see in this country.
