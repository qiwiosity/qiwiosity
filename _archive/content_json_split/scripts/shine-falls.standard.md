# Shine Falls — standard (100–120s)

Shine Falls is the waterfall most Hawke's Bay locals haven't been to. It's hidden in the Maungaharuru Range, inland from the coast between Napier and Wairoa, inside DOC's Boundary Stream Mainland Island — a reserve where the department has done decades of intense predator control to bring back the forest birds. You're walking into a place that sounds, genuinely, like New Zealand used to sound: tūī, bellbirds, kererū, sometimes a kākā.

The track from Heays Access Road is about an hour and three-quarters one way, through kanuka, kawakawa, kōwhai and tītoki. It's not difficult, but it's remote. There's no reception. There's almost always no one there.

And then, around a corner, the falls. Fifty-eight metres — the tallest in Hawke's Bay — falling clean off a limestone cliff. A "horsetail" waterfall, meaning the water is pinched narrow at the top and fans out over grooves worn in the rock. At the base, a shallow pool with native long-fin eels in it.

A note you need to hear: Cyclone Gabrielle, in February 2023, was catastrophic for this valley. Bridges were destroyed. The track was ripped up in places. DOC has been reopening sections in stages, and conditions change. Check the DOC website before you set out — don't trust an app, including this one, to be up to date on a track this exposed to weather.

And the "mainland island" name, while we're here, is worth understanding. It's a DOC programme where one patch of unfenced forest gets trapped, poisoned, and monitored so intensely that it behaves like a pest-free offshore island. Boundary Stream was one of the original six, set up in the 1990s. You're walking through one of the quiet victories of New Zealand conservation.
