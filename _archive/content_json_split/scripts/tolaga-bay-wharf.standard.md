# Tolaga Bay Wharf — standard (105–125s)

Tolaga Bay doesn't have a proper harbour. It has a long, shallow, open beach facing the Pacific. The local name is Ūawa — the estuary. For sheep and wool farmers on this coast in the 1920s, that was a problem. Ships couldn't come in close. Everything had to be barged out to waiting vessels, slowly, expensively, and in weather conditions that were usually trying to kill everyone involved.

So in 1926, marine engineer Cyrus Williams drew up a plan the locals agreed to pay for: a reinforced concrete wharf long enough to reach deep water, out past the swell. The target was 660 metres. Construction started that year. It went wrong almost immediately — storms damaged the works, the approach had to be rerouted, and the harbour board had to borrow another £20,000 to finish. The opening, with the Minister of Marine in attendance, was on 22 November 1929.

At its peak, in 1936, 133 ships a year worked the wharf. Wool out. Supplies and machinery in. Coastal boats like the Kopara were the lifeline of the East Coast before the roads were sealed.

Then the roads got sealed. The coasters stopped calling. In 1966 the Kopara made the last sailing. The wharf became a memory, and started falling to pieces.

What saved it is unusual. In the 1990s a group of locals — the Tolaga Bay Save the Wharf Charitable Trust — decided they couldn't let it go. They've been repairing it section by section for three decades, and it's now Category 1 Heritage New Zealand listed.

Walk out to the end. It takes about eight minutes each way. You can see fish in the water below. On a good day you'll feel the concrete flex a little under the swell — it's designed to. A few hundred metres to the north, the Cooks Cove walkway takes you to where James Cook's Endeavour took shelter in October 1769 and the botanist Joseph Banks first got a good look at New Zealand flora. But the wharf is the main event.
