# Napier–Taupō Road (SH5) — standard (140–160s)

The road you're about to drive is officially State Highway 5. Locals call it the Napier–Taupō. A hundred and forty-two kilometres, two hours on a good day, four if anything goes wrong — and things go wrong here often.

Start with how old it is. The route began as a Māori inland track, connecting the Heretaunga plains with the vast interior of the central North Island — the Kaingaroa, Lake Taupō, and the volcanic plateau. When the missionary William Colenso walked it in 1845, heading inland, he was following a path that had been used for centuries.

The coaching era started in 1872. On the first of January that year, Cobb & Co launched a Napier-to-Taupō coach service. It was a two-day trip. Horses were changed at Petane — now Eskdale — then up to Te Pōhue for lunch. Then the descent to the Mohaka River, another change at Tarawera, and overnight in a coaching hotel. The road wasn't a road — it was a cut through dense bush, a sequence of saddles and river fords, and a set of hotels that routinely burnt down.

Motorised traffic made things worse before they got better. The two big climbs — Turangakumu and Titiokura — were so hard on trucks that, before the Second World War, transport companies used to stash spare gearboxes at the summits so drivers could swap one out when theirs failed. In the 1950s a new surveying technology called photogrammetry let engineers redesign the alignment; they lowered the Turangakumu summit by a hundred metres. The last long unsealed stretch — the Runanga Deviation — wasn't opened until 1972.

The 1931 Hawke's Bay earthquake rerouted the end of the road. It raised the land at Bay View by metres, moved the mouth of the Esk River, and forced a realignment into Napier that's still in place today.

And then — on the 14th of February 2023 — Cyclone Gabrielle. Slips closed the road in multiple places. Marshall's Bridge near Te Pōhue had its supports washed out. The official detour was five hours long: south to Woodville, up the middle of the island, and around. SH5 reopened to daylight-only traffic on the 20th of March, and to full 24-hour use weeks after that.

Every stop you see on this drive — Eskdale, Te Pōhue, Tarawera, the Mohaka Bridge, Opepe — sits on top of that stacked history. Keep your eyes on the road. But when you stop, the stops are worth it.
