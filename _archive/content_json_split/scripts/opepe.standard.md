# Opepe — standard (120–140s)

You're driving through the site of one of the most consequential ambushes in the New Zealand Wars. It's easy to miss. The Opepe Historic Reserve is signposted off the left, about fifteen kilometres east of Taupō, and it contains nine graves in a small clearing in the bush.

The story begins in early June 1869. Te Kooti Arikirangi — a Rongowhakaata leader, escaped political prisoner, and founder of the Ringatū faith — had been fighting a mobile guerrilla campaign across the central and eastern North Island for nearly two years. Government forces were chasing him. By the first week of June he was in the Taupō region, trying to rally support.

On the 6th of June a detachment of fourteen troopers from the Bay of Plenty Volunteer Cavalry rode into Opepe, an old forest clearing used by travellers. They were tired, they were dismounted, and they made a serious mistake: they took off their boots and their tunics, and set no proper watch.

Just after dawn on the 7th of June, Te Kooti's men came in through the trees. It was over in minutes. Nine of the fourteen troopers were killed. A few escaped into the bush — one, trooper George Crosswell, reportedly ran for kilometres without boots and with almost no clothing, eventually reaching Runanga Station and raising the alarm.

The ambush was a serious military and political shock. It hardened the government campaign against Te Kooti, which chased him through the Urewera until 1872, when he escaped into the King Country and lived there until a pardon in 1883. He continued to lead the Ringatū church — which still has tens of thousands of followers today — until his death in 1893.

The graves at Opepe were maintained by local farmers for decades and are now a Department of Conservation historic reserve, enclosed by a low white fence. A small stand of native bush surrounds them. It takes about twenty minutes to walk the loop. If you stop, you'll find it quiet. It always is.
