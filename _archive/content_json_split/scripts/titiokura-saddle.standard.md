# Titiokura Saddle — standard (95–115s)

Pull over at the top if you can. This is Titiokura Saddle — seven hundred and thirty-six metres up, on the ridgeline of the Maungaharuru Range, and on a clear day it is one of the great understated views on the North Island.

Turn east and you're looking back down the whole of Hawke's Bay — the Heretaunga plains, Napier on the coast, the Pacific beyond. On a summer evening you can watch cloud climb the ranges behind you and pour, slow motion, down toward the sea. Turn the other way and you're facing the inland country: the Kaweka Forest Park, the long folded ridges, and the Mohaka River valley disappearing off toward the volcanic plateau.

This saddle has a harder history than the view suggests. It was part of the original Māori inland track, used for centuries before European arrival. When the missionary William Colenso walked from Napier inland in the 1840s, he climbed this saddle on a footpath. In the 1870s a stockade was built up here as part of the colonial government's chain of outposts along the coaching road, one of which — Opepe, further west — would become the site of a disastrous ambush.

The climb up to where you're standing was so hard on early motor transport that before the Second World War, freight companies stashed spare gearboxes at the summit — genuinely, literally spare gearboxes, sitting under tarps, waiting for the next truck that arrived with a dead one. Major realignments in the 1950s, using the then-new technology of aerial photogrammetry, cut the summit of the next climb west — Turangakumu — by a full hundred metres.

When you drive on, you'll feel the road drop away and then the next big climb lift you up again. It's still an honest piece of road. Keep your eyes up.
