# Lake Taupō — standard (125–145s)

You're coming over the rise now, and you'll see it: Lake Taupō. Six hundred and sixteen square kilometres of water, about the size of Singapore, sitting in the middle of the North Island at the ankle of the volcanic plateau. Most people look at it and think "big lake." What you're actually looking at is the caldera of a supervolcano.

The basin began to form around three hundred thousand years ago, but the shape you see today was carved by an eruption twenty-five and a half thousand years ago called the Oruanui. It is one of the largest eruptions in the last hundred thousand years anywhere on Earth. It threw out around twelve hundred cubic kilometres of material — enough to bury the entire North Island a metre deep. The hole it left is the lake.

The volcano has erupted twenty-eight times since. The most recent big one was the Hatepe eruption, dated by recent ice-core work to around the year 232. It was a VEI 7 event — among the most violent eruptions of the last five thousand years on the planet. Witnesses on the other side of the world, in Rome and China, recorded unusually red skies that year. The pumice deposits from that single eruption are what you've been driving on for the last thirty kilometres.

Ngāti Tūwharetoa are the iwi of the lake. Their ancestor Ngātoro-i-rangi is said to have stood on Mount Tauhara — the mountain on your right as you come into town — and thrown a tōtara tree into the lake to create the passage at Taupō-nui-a-Tia. The full name of the lake means "the great cloak of Tia," a reference to an earlier ancestor.

The lake drains north through the Waikato River, the longest river in New Zealand. You'll see it in a few minutes — a wide, very blue channel pouring out toward the falls at Huka.

If you have time, the lakefront at Taupō township is worth half an hour. The water is clearer than you'd expect for a lake this size. It's also, on a calm morning, one of the most peaceful views in the country.
