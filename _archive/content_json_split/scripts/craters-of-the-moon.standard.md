# Craters of the Moon — standard (105–125s)

Craters of the Moon — known in Māori as Karapiti — is one of the strangest geothermal fields in the country, and the strangest thing about it is how recent most of it is.

Before 1950, the area was a mild steaming hillside. There was some low-level activity — hissing vents, a few warm patches — but nothing dramatic. Then in 1958 the Wairakei Geothermal Power Station came online a few kilometres north. It was only the second geothermal power plant in the world and at the time the largest. To drive its turbines, Wairakei pulled enormous volumes of hot water and steam out of the ground.

The effect on the wider thermal system was immediate. Pressure underground dropped. Groundwater levels fell. In places like Karapiti, where the rock was thin and the hot zone was close to the surface, the fall in pressure allowed the remaining water to flash to steam. The ground, in short, blew up.

New craters opened. Steam vents appeared overnight. The hillside you walk across today — an unstable, cracked landscape of hissing holes and bubbling mud pots — is almost entirely a consequence of human activity. It is, in that specific sense, an industrial accident on a geological scale. It is also, to be clear, safe to visit: DOC maintains a two-kilometre boardwalk loop through the field, with platforms over the hotter zones.

A couple of things worth knowing before you walk: wear shoes, not jandals — the ground temperature can reach boiling. Stick to the boardwalk — the crust is genuinely thin in places. And pick a time when it's cool and overcast if you can. The steam is at its most dramatic when the air above is cold.

What you're visiting is a landscape that is still changing. New vents open every few years. If you come back in ten years, some parts of it will look different again.
