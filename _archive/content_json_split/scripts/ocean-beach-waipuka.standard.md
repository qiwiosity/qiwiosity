# Ocean Beach / Waipuka — standard (110–130s)

The road over the hill drops you onto one of the most honest beaches in New Zealand. No pier. No promenade. Long golden sand, heavy surf, a scatter of old baches, and the Pacific doing exactly what it wants.

To the small Māori community who've lived here for generations, this is Waipuka. The beach sits between two of the most significant features of the Hawke's Bay coast — Cape Kidnappers to the north, Waimarama and the Motu-o-Kura islets to the south.

In 1838, at the shoulder of the bay, a whaler called William Morris set up the Rangaiika shore station. This was the violent peak of the Pacific whaling economy: Māori crews and European crews working from the beach, boiling blubber, trading muskets and potatoes. The station was short-lived. Most of these coastal operations were gone within fifteen years as the whale populations collapsed.

In 1887, a coastal steamer called the *Go Ahead* came to grief along this stretch. You can still find the iron boiler wedged into the rocks — a hundred and forty years of swell hasn't shifted it. It's one of those artefacts that tells you exactly how much force is moving past here, constantly, most days of the year.

Today, Ocean Beach is surfer country. The break is sand-bottomed, the peaks move around with the weather, and it works in a lot of swells. There are summer surf lifesaving patrols, because the rip off the middle of the beach is no joke — one of the stronger rips on the east coast. Swim between the flags. If there are no flags, walk.

Two rock arches along the southern end at low tide. Nesting shags on the cliffs. If you stand here at dawn with coffee and no one else around, you understand why the people who grew up at Waipuka don't really want it written up too enthusiastically.
