# Cape Kidnappers / Te Kauwae-a-Māui — standard (110–130s)

Start with the shape. The cliff curves away from you like a hook — and that's not an accident of description. To Māori, this is Te Kauwae-a-Māui, the jawbone of Māui. In the old story, Māui goes fishing with his brothers, casts a line with a hook made from his grandmother Murirangawhenua's jawbone, and hauls up the North Island — Te Ika-a-Māui, the fish of Māui. The cape you're looking at is the hook still in its mouth.

The English name is younger, and sadder. On the fifteenth of October 1769, James Cook's Endeavour was anchored offshore. A waka came out to trade. On board Cook's ship was a young Tahitian boy named Taiata, the servant of the navigator Tupaia. The men in the waka — a party led by Te Rangikoianake of Ngāi Te Whatuiāpiti — saw a young Polynesian boy on a foreign ship and believed, understandably, that he was a captive. They took him. Cook's crew fired. Taiata swam back. Cook wrote "Cape Kidnappers" in his journal, and the name stuck for two and a half centuries.

Two stories, same piece of rock. Both true in their own ways.

The wildlife is the third story. Around 6,500 pairs of Australasian gannets nest on the cliff in front of you, laying eggs in October and fledging chicks by April. It's the most accessible mainland gannet colony in the world — you can stand within a few metres of them. They've been coming back here, year after year, since at least the 1870s.

Three ways to visit. One: walk from Clifton Beach at low tide — about three hours each way, and the tide traps people every summer, so check the timing carefully. Two: book a tractor trailer tour up the beach. Three: the overland route through Cape Kidnappers Station. Whichever you choose, arrive quietly. The gannets are used to us; they're not tame.
